Acoustic-Wave Energy Beaming

Focused Ultrasound for Long-Distance Power Transmission

waves1. Introduction

Acoustic-wave energy beaming uses phased arrays of ultrasonic transducers to generate tightly focused beams of mechanical energy that propagate through air or water, delivering power over distances without physical conductors. By converting electrical input into high-intensity acoustic waves and reconverting at the receiver via piezoelectric or thermoacoustic transducers, this method enables wireless power delivery for remote sensors, drones, and underwater vehicles.

This theoretical design document details physical principles, governing equations, system architecture, phased-array beamforming, energy conversion, fabrication methods, operational protocols, applications, advantages, challenges, and future enhancements.

lightbulb2. Background & Motivation

  • Limitations of electromagnetic beaming: Regulatory power density limits, line-of-sight constraints, RF interference.
  • Acoustic advantages: Higher allowable intensities in certain media, lower environmental scattering, safe frequencies above human hearing.
  • Applications: Powering remote IoT devices, UAV charging, underwater unmanned vehicles, distributed sensor networks.

science3. Physical Principles

3.1. Acoustic Wave Equation

Pressure field $p(\mathbf{r},t)$ satisfies:

$$ \nabla^2 p - \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial t^2} = 0, $$

with sound speed $c$ in medium.

In harmonic steady state $p(\mathbf{r},t)=P(\mathbf{r}) e^{-i\omega t}$:

$$ (\nabla^2 + k^2) P(\mathbf{r}) = 0, \quad k = \frac{\omega}{c}. $$

3.2. Intensity and Power Flux

Time-average acoustic intensity:

$$ I = \frac{1}{2} \mathrm{Re}(p v^*) = \frac{|P|^2}{2\rho c}, $$

where $v$ is particle velocity, $\rho$ density. Power delivered to receiver area $A$:

$$ P_{recv} = I\, A\, \eta_{conv}, $$

with conversion efficiency $\eta_{conv}$.

3.3. Phased-Array Beamforming

For an array of $N$ transducers at positions $\mathbf{r}_n$ with amplitude $A_n$ and phase $\phi_n$, the field at target $\mathbf{R}$:

$$ P(\mathbf{R}) = \sum_{n=1}^N A_n \frac{e^{ik|\mathbf{R}-\mathbf{r}_n| + i\phi_n}}{|\mathbf{R}-\mathbf{r}_n|}. $$

To focus at $\mathbf{R}_0$, choose $\phi_n = -k|\mathbf{R}_0 - \mathbf{r}_n|$.

3.4. Beamwidth and Focal Gain

Diffraction-limited focal spot radius ~ $w_0\approx1.22\,\frac{\lambda f}{D}$, where $D$ array diameter, $f$ focal distance. Focal gain:

$$ G = \frac{4\pi A}{\lambda^2}, \quad A = \frac{\pi D^2}{4}. $$

3.5. Attenuation and Medium Effects

Acoustic attenuation coefficient $\alpha(f)$ gives pressure decay:

$$ P(z) = P_0 e^{-\alpha z}, $$

with frequency-dependent $\alpha$ (dB/m). Trade-off between frequency (focusing) and attenuation.

  1. Signal Generator: Produces multi-channel drive signals with programmable phase and amplitude.
  2. Power Amplifiers: High-voltage pulsers driving piezoelectric elements.
  3. Phased-Array Transducer: 1D/2D array of ultrasonic emitters (e.g., 40 kHz–1 MHz).
  4. Receiving Transducer: Piezoelectric or Kapitza conductors reconverting acoustic to electrical energy.
  5. Power Conditioning: Rectifier, DC-DC converter to stabilize output voltage.

precision_manufacturing5. Fabrication & Materials

  1. Piezoelectric Elements: PZT or PVDF membranes diced into array elements.
  2. Array Substrate: Printed-circuit board with electrode traces and matching network.
  3. Acoustic Matching Layers: Quarter-wave impedance transformers to minimize reflection.
  4. Encapsulation: Waterproofing for underwater use; acoustic windows for air.
  5. Receiver Design: Tuned resonant transducer matched to beam frequency.

play_circle_outline6. Operational Protocol

  1. Calibration: Measure array element phase delays and correct for path differences.
  2. Beamforming: Compute phase map for desired focal point(s).
  3. Transmission: Apply drive signals; monitor power levels and ambient reflections.
  4. Tracking: For moving receiver, update focal coordinates in real time.
  5. Energy Harvesting: Receiver synchronizes to acoustic frequency for optimal conversion.

apps8. Applications & Advantages

  • IoT Sensor Networks: Continuous powering of distributed sensors in inaccessible areas.
  • Drone Recharging: Tetherless in-flight battery topping for UAVs.
  • Underwater Vehicles: Wireless energy delivery to AUVs and sensors.
  • Medical Devices: Transcutaneous power for implants via ultrasound.

Advantages:

  1. Non-ionizing: Safe ultrasound frequencies.
  2. Beam steering: Electronic control without moving parts.
  3. Medium penetration: Better focus in water and certain solids compared to RF.
  4. Flexibility: Multi-focal beams for powering multiple targets.

report_problem9. Challenges & Future Directions

  • Efficiency limits: Losses in beam transmission and conversion; optimizing matching layers.
  • Safety and bioeffects: Ensuring intensities below cavitation and thermal thresholds.
  • Array complexity: Large channel counts for fine beam control; cost and power appetite.
  • Dynamic environments: Adapting to turbulence, motion, and multipath reflections.

Future work: adaptive beamforming with feedback from receiver, metamaterial lenses for compact focusing, high-frequency arrays for mm-scale resolution.
